03 · R² Analysis

Why the R² Improvements Matter

Two sequential currency adjustments — each improving model fit substantially. Both use MSCI Nordic as the market factor throughout; the improvements come from currency conversion, not factor choice.

Converting the MSCI Nordic market factor from EUR to NOK (and switching the risk-free rate from EUR RF to NIBOR) increases R² by 15–22 percentage points across all funds — better currency alignment with NOK-denominated fund returns immediately improves model fit. Then converting the five FF factors (SMB, HML, RMW, CMA, WML) from USD to NOK adds another 8–22 percentage points on top. Together, the two currency adjustments take average R² from ~52% up to ~77%, meaning the final model explains the vast majority of fund return variation — and yet even with this precision, not all funds generate statistically significant alpha.

04 · Key Findings

What the Data Tells Us

01
Passive investing dominates — 7 of 10 funds trail the benchmark
The VINX benchmark (8.47% annualised, Sharpe 0.407) outperforms 7 of 10 funds on return and 8 of 10 on Sharpe ratio. Under CAPM, zero funds produce statistically significant alpha — the highest |t-stat| is 1.40. This is consistent with efficient market theory for a liquid, developed Nordic market, where information advantages are rapidly arbitraged away.
0/10 sig. CAPM alpha7/10 below benchmarkBenchmark Sharpe: 0.407
02
Multi-factor model changes the picture — 7/10 significant in FF6 FX Adj.
The central analytical finding. Moving from CAPM to the fully currency-adjusted FF6 model takes significant alpha from 0 to 7 out of 10 funds. CAPM was penalising factor tilts as if they were uncompensated risk. Funds with strong growth tilts (negative HML) were being charged for "value risk" they were not actually taking. Stripping out these factor exposures reveals the true manager residual. The progression from 0 to 1 to 4 to 7 significant funds across the four models is the central analytical story of this analysis.

DNB Nordic Small Cap — strongest alpha (t = 3.74) but worst conventional metrics
Annualised FF6 FX alpha of +17.2% (t = 3.74) — the highest result in the sample. Driven by a massive SMB loading of +1.084 (t = 5.64). The fund ranks poorly on raw return (5.93%) and has the highest max drawdown (−30.6%), yet demonstrates strong statistically significant skill once factor tilts are accounted for. This is the clearest illustration of why raw returns are a misleading measure of managerial ability.

05 · Methodology

Data & Methods

Sample & Universe
10 actively managed Nordic equity funds (NAV in NOK). April 2021 – March 2026. T = 60 monthly observations. Benchmark: VINXBCAPNOKNI (Nasdaq VINX Benchmark Cap NOK Net Index).
Risk-Free Rate
Norwegian NIBOR (monthly end-of-period). Applied to compute all fund and benchmark excess returns. Appropriate given all NAVs and benchmark are NOK-denominated.
Factor Model
European Fama-French 5-factor + Momentum (WML) from Kenneth French's data library. These factors are computed from a USD perspective — USD→NOK conversion applied for the currency-adjusted specifications.
Nordic Market Factor
MSCI Nordic Countries Net Return Index (EUR) converted to NOK using monthly EUR/NOK spot rate changes. Replaces the European FF Mkt-RF in both CAPM and FF6 regressions.
MSCI Nordic → NOK conversion:
MSCI_NOK = (1 + MSCI_EUR) × (1 + EUR/NOK_Δ) − 1
MKT_RF_adj = MSCI_NOK − NIBOR

FF Factor conversion (USD → NOK):
Factor_NOK = (1 + Factor_USD) × (1 + USD/NOK_Δ) − 1 − NIBOR

Four regressions per fund (40 total): (1) CAPM No FX Adj — European FF Mkt-RF  |  (2) CAPM FX Adj — MSCI Nordic NOK  |  (3) FF6 No FX Adj — Nordic mkt + raw FF factors  |  (4) FF6 FX Adj — Nordic mkt + USD→NOK FF factors

Sep 2022
Maximum Drawdown — All Funds
Every fund hit its maximum drawdown simultaneously during the European rate-hiking cycle. Range: −17.5% (Alfred Berg) to −30.6% (DNB Small Cap). Systematic risk dominated entirely.
Mar 2025
Synchronised Market Crash
Benchmark fell −8.47%. Nearly every fund fell 7–10% in the same month — confirming that a single-region active portfolio provides no diversification against systematic shocks.
Q1 2026

−9.0%, −8.6%, −5.1% in three consecutive months while the benchmark was approximately flat. Low benchmark correlation (0.752) reflects concentrated idiosyncratic bets that failed persistently.
06 · Investment Recommendation

Should You Invest in These Funds?

Based on five years of performance data, CAPM and FF6 regressions, and risk-adjusted metrics — a frank assessment of all 10 funds.

The overarching conclusion: The passive benchmark (VINXBCAPNOKNI) outperforms 7 of 10 funds on raw return, 8 of 10 on Sharpe ratio, and zero funds produce statistically significant CAPM alpha over the 5-year period. The most defensible recommendation for a Norwegian investor seeking Nordic equity exposure is to buy the index — lower cost, better risk-adjusted returns, no manager risk. If active management is required, the three funds below represent the least objectionable choices based on the evidence.

✓ Recommend

FondsFinans Utbytte B — First choice
The only fund with a positive Information Ratio (+0.050) and best-in-class on Sharpe (0.475), Sortino (0.720), raw return (8.85%), and cumulative return (+49.8%). Significant alpha in FF6 FX Adj (t = 2.19). Its dividend-yield mandate appears to deliver a genuine quality tilt with mild downside protection. If you must invest actively in Nordic equities, this is the clearest choice.

A note on DNB Nordic Small Cap: This fund deserves special treatment. Its raw return (5.93%) and conventional metrics are poor, yet it has the strongest statistical alpha in the entire sample (FF6 FX Adj: t = 3.74, ann. α = +17.2%). The alpha survives controlling for its large small-cap (SMB = 1.084) and aggressive investment (CMA = −0.471) tilts. If an investor specifically wants small-cap Nordic exposure and has the risk tolerance for −30.6% drawdowns, the manager appears to add genuine value within that mandate. It is therefore neither a clear recommend nor a clear avoid — it depends entirely on the investor's mandate and risk appetite.
